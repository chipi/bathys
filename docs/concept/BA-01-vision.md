## Why Bathys exists

The ocean covers 71% of the Earth's surface. It produces half the oxygen we breathe, absorbs 90% of the excess heat we generate, and regulates the climate of every place on land. It is the origin of life, the highway of human civilisation, and the last great frontier of scientific exploration.

It is also almost completely invisible to most people.

We have better maps of the surface of Mars than of our own ocean floor. Of the estimated 700,000 to one million marine species, roughly a third remain formally undescribed by science. The hadal trenches — the deepest places on Earth — have been visited by more people than have walked on the Moon, but barely.

Bathys exists to change that relationship. Not through advocacy, and not through alarm — through wonder.

The name comes from the Greek *bathys*: deep. It is the root of bathyscaphe, bathymetry, the bathypelagic zone. It is what you become when you go below.

## What Bathys is

Bathys is a learning laboratory for the ocean — a place where anyone, from a child doing a school project to a technical diver planning a trimix dive to a researcher tracing the history of deep-sea exploration, can find something true and beautiful and surprising.

It is built around six ideas:

**The globe is the entry point.** The ocean is one connected system. You begin with the whole planet, see the bathymetric topology of the seafloor rendered in real data, watch the great current systems move, and zoom in to any point of interest from there. Every other section of Bathys is reachable from the globe.

**Depth changes everything.** The visual language of Bathys shifts as you go deeper — from sunlit turquoise at the surface, through rich blue-green at reef depth, into deep indigo at the mesopelagic, and near-black in the abyss. The interface is tuned to the depth context of what you are looking at. Science feels different from diving, and the deep sea feels different from the coral triangle. This is intentional.

**Science is primary, beauty is the delivery mechanism.** Every number in Bathys traces back to a paper, a dataset, or a primary source. The decompression simulator runs real Bühlmann ZHL-16 computations. The species data comes from OBIS and the IUCN Red List. The MPA polygons are live WDPA data. But the way all of this is presented — the custom illustrations, the interactive depth cross-sections, the animated current flows — is designed to make rigour feel like discovery, not homework.

**Exploration is human.** The history of going underwater is one of the great stories of the 20th century. Cousteau and Calypso. Walsh and Piccard in the Trieste. Ballard and the Titanic. Cameron alone at Challenger Deep. Ballesta in his illuminated deep. These stories live in Bathys not as footnotes but as chapters — with photographs, with context, with the vessels and instruments and people who made them possible. Ocean science is not anonymous.

**The ocean is global.** The science comes from JAMSTEC, IFREMER, AWI, MBARI and Shirshov as much as from Scripps and Woods Hole. The explorers come from France, Russia, Japan, Colombia, Sri Lanka, Australia and Germany as much as from the United States. The biodiversity is centred in Indonesia and the Eastern Pacific, not the Caribbean. Bathys reflects the ocean as it actually is.

**Protection is part of the story.** 14,830 marine protected areas cover roughly 8% of the ocean. The target is 30% by 2030. The gap between those two numbers is one of the most consequential facts in ocean science. Bathys shows where every protected area is, what it protects, and how effective that protection actually is. This is not a conservation campaign — it is an honest account.

## Who Bathys is for

Bathys has three audiences, each real and each distinct:

**The curious non-specialist.** Someone who watched a Blue Planet episode and wanted to go deeper. Someone who dives recreationally and wants to understand what they are seeing. Someone who encountered a news story about deep-sea mining or coral bleaching and wanted the full picture. Bathys meets them at the globe and takes them as far as they want to go.

**The diver.** Recreational, technical, freediver. The Dive Lab gives them a decompression simulator built on real published algorithms — Bühlmann ZHL-16, VPM-B, RGBM — that explains the physics of what is happening in their tissues on every dive. The world's top 100 dive sites, mapped, with galleries and species lists. The physics of light, pressure, sound, and gas at depth explained with the specificity a diver needs. This is not a dive planning tool — it is the science behind the dive, made beautiful.

**The student and researcher.** Someone doing marine biology or oceanography who wants a reference that is both scientifically rigorous and genuinely navigable. Every claim cited, every dataset sourced, every algorithm traceable to primary literature. The Credits and Library pages are a serious academic resource, not an afterthought.

## What makes Bathys different

There are ocean education websites. There are dive planning apps. There are scientific data portals. There are natural history museum exhibits about marine life.

Bathys is none of these things, and all of them at once.

The combination that does not currently exist — leading-edge interactive technology, genuine scientific rigour, global perspective, personal photographic content from a working underwater photographer, and a design sensibility serious enough to make complex science feel like the best magazine you have ever read — is the combination Bathys is built to be.

The orrery proved the model works: a single-person project, built carefully, can produce something that feels like it came from a major institution. Bathys applies that same rigour to the ocean, which is a bigger subject, a broader audience, and a more urgent story.

---

## The design soul

The orrery was space: dark, precise, amber and gold, the aesthetic of an instrument.

Bathys is water: alive, layered, shifting. Light through the surface at noon. The blue-black at 200 metres. The total dark below 1000 metres interrupted only by bioluminescence. The visual register of the interface responds to depth — not as a gimmick but as a way of making the user feel where they are.

The typography is clean and editorial. The illustrations are custom, not stock. The photography is real — not curated from agencies but from the ocean floor itself, shot by the person who built the project. This is a place that has been to the places it is describing.

## What success looks like

A technical diver runs the decompression simulator and recognises the Bühlmann compartment curves from their own dive computer.

A marine biology student uses the Flora & Fauna species cards as a reference and finds the citations accurate and the OBIS data current.

A twelve-year-old arrives at the globe, zooms into Raja Ampat, and spends forty minutes reading about coral.

A researcher at IFREMER finds their institution credited accurately and their data properly attributed.

Someone who has never dived in their life watches the animated thermohaline conveyor belt and understands, for the first time, why the Atlantic stays warm.

Jacques Cousteau's granddaughter, if she ever sees it, recognises the spirit that drove Calypso.
